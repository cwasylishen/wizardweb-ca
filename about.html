The Story

He is not a developer who learned marketing. He is a marketer who learned to build.

Clinton Wasylishen spent over three decades in the trenches of real sales. Not digital sales. Belly-to-belly, face-to-face, close-or-go-home sales. He built multiple seven-figure businesses from the ground up, including a bootcamp company and four brick-and-mortar gyms. He has hired sales teams, trained them, fired them, and rebuilt them. He knows what moves a customer from interested to convinced.

When he decided to build websites, he did not come at it as a technologist. He came at it as someone who has spent his whole career studying one thing: what makes people say yes. That changes everything. The way a page opens, the way a headline lands, the way trust gets built one sentence at a time. None of that comes from a coding bootcamp. It comes from years on the floor.

Clinton is also a father, an endurance athlete who runs ultramarathons and 24-hour events, and someone who rebuilt his life and business after major setbacks. He is now based in Costa Rica, working with clients across Western Canada and beyond, bringing the same directness and clarity to every website he builds that he brought to every sales floor he ever worked.

Every headline is written to convert. Every page is structured to rank. Every design choice is made with one question: does this help the customer say yes?

The Philosophy

"A website without a story is just a brochure nobody asked for."

Clinton Wasylishen, WizardWeb

Most web agencies focus on code. WizardWeb focuses on the customer, the story, the conversion. The tech is the vehicle, not the destination.

Speed, SEO, and mobile-first are not selling points here. They are the floor. Every site ships with a 100/100 PageSpeed score, semantic HTML, schema markup baked in from line one, and clean code that Google can actually read. That is baseline. You should not have to pay extra for that.

What separates a good website from one that actually grows your business is whether it can tell your story in a way that makes a stranger trust you enough to pick up the phone. That is the harder thing to build. And that is exactly what Clinton has spent 30 years learning how to do.
